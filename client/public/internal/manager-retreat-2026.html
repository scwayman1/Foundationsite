The design premise

Agency needs a method.

The retreat is about more than empowering people to act. Managers need a repeatable way to define the real problem, map the dependencies, anticipate consequences, build the right coalition, and make the case for a decision.

“A good idea is not enough. Is the timing right? Is the team right? What does it affect? Who needs to be with us?”

Afternoon working lab

Name the friction. Find the system. Make the move.

01 • Surface it

Put the friction on the wall.

Use Padlet or color-coded sticky notes to capture what absorbs time, slows service, creates confusion, or keeps repeating. Do not mistake the first complaint for the actual problem.

AskWhat is happening repeatedly—and who feels it first?
02 • Map it

See the decision system.

Separate symptoms from root causes. Identify barriers, dependencies, unintended consequences, influencers, and the part the group can actually control.

AskWhat changes somewhere else if we solve this here?
03 • Rank it

Choose what deserves the room.

Rank the work by student and service impact, control, return on investment, readiness, and the value of a small win versus a harder high-impact move.

AskIs this the right problem, at the right time, with the right people?
04 • Commit

Make the next move visible.

Name the owner, coalition, first action, escalation required, and the evidence that will tell the group whether to continue, adjust, or stop.

AskWhat can we publicly commit to—and what decision do we need from others?
Stories that frame the work

Coastline has done this before.

The examples are not nostalgia. They are evidence that good decisions require a clear problem, the courage to choose differently, and the judgment to change course when conditions change.

01

Start Coastline differently

The founding story: when the existing path did not serve the purpose, Coastline chose to build a different model rather than wait for consensus that would never come.

02

CPORT → Canvas

Coastline built what it needed when the available learning systems were not good enough, then moved to Canvas when the platform, state support, and return on investment made realignment the smarter move.

03

Online tutoring: early is not always wrong

An early online tutoring effort worked in concept but arrived before students had the bandwidth and readiness. The underlying need later became viable when the conditions changed.

04

Apollo 13: solve with what is here

The day’s team challenge makes the constraint visible: define the problem, use the resources available, coordinate the team, and make the best case for the solution.

What leaves the room

A decision record, not a complaint log.

Capture the work that earned a next move: the underlying problem, its impact, the people and systems it touches, what the team controls, and the precise ask that needs escalation.

Facilitator posture

Do not let the group stop at “the district will not let us” or “we need more people.” Help them describe the real problem, the decision system around it, and the strongest credible next move.

Questions that keep the work honest

Who feels this first: students, staff, or another campus?
Is this the root problem, a symptom, or a barrier?
What changes elsewhere if we solve it here?
Is this the right time, team, coalition, and case?
