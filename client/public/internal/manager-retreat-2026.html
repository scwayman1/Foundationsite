The point of the day

A retreat with receipts.

We are not here to generate a prettier list of problems. We are here to identify work worth doing, decide what we control, and leave with a small number of commitments that make service better and capacity stronger.

“Notice the problem. Name it plainly. Work the part you control. Bring the rest back with a real recommendation.”

The workshop

Four moves. No theater.

01 • See it

Bring a real point of friction.

Not a complaint. A specific moment where a student, colleague, or customer gets slowed down, confused, or sent in circles.

AskWhat work keeps repeating because the system is asking people to compensate for it?
02 • Bound it

Separate the whole problem from your part.

Some issues need policy, money, or a different system. That is fine. Identify the part the team can begin without waiting for perfect conditions.

AskWhat do we control this week? What needs a decision above us?
03 • Test it

Make the first move small enough to start.

A useful test has an owner, a date, a visible before/after, and a way to stop if it is not helping.

AskWhat can we test in 30 days without pretending we have solved everything?
04 • Share it

Show the work and make the ask.

Bring back the result, not just the idea. If you need help, name the decision and the person who can make it.

AskWhat evidence would make a reasonable person say: keep going?
Useful stories, not hero worship

Examples with a point.

Each story is there to open a conversation about constraints, not to suggest that every problem has a dramatic answer.

Apollo 13

The lesson is not the movie. It is the discipline of defining the constraint, using what is available, and keeping the team focused on the next workable move.

03

Facilitator posture

Keep the day useful. Do not let people hide in abstractions, complaints, or hypothetical solutions. Bring it back to a real person, a real moment, and a next move.

Questions that keep it honest

Who feels this problem first?
What would a better version look like in plain English?
What part can we move without waiting for everyone else?
What would make us stop, adjust, or scale the test?

Parking lot rule

A problem is allowed to be bigger than the room. If it needs policy, budget, or executive sponsorship, capture the decision needed and who owns that decision. Do not pretend it is a team action item.
